Case Study: Galaxy Rotation Curve

The Dark Matter Problem

Mathematical Description of the Problem

Standard Expected Behavior (Newton/GR)

The centripetal force (F_c = mv²/r) is equated with the gravitational force (F_g = GmM(r)/r²). Orbital velocity is derived as:

v(r)² = GM(r)/r

At the edge of a galaxy, where M(r) ≈ const, the expected behavior is:

v(r) ∝ 1/√r
(Velocity should decrease with distance)

Observed Anomaly

In reality, observations show that v(r) ≈ const in the outer regions of galaxies.

Standard Explanation

To resolve this, a hypothetical dark matter halo is introduced with a mass distribution of M_dark(r) ∝ r.

The Dephaze Model Derivation

In the Dephaze model, motion is governed by an effective potential (U_eff) composed of the standard gravitational potential and the intrinsic potential of the Dephaze field.

Ueff(r) = Ugravity(r) + Udephaze(r)

Source of the Dephaze Potential

At large distances, the Φ³ projective operator dominates, projecting the galaxy's field into a stable topology that creates an effective potential. This potential asymptotically approaches a logarithmic form:

Udephaze(r) ≈ C * ln(r)

Derivation of the Velocity Curve

The square of the orbital velocity is proportional to the gradient of the effective potential: v(r)² = r * dU_eff/dr. This leads to:

v(r)² = GM(r)/r + C

Asymptotic Behavior

In the outer regions of a galaxy (as r → ∞), the GM(r)/r term approaches zero, and the constant C from the Dephaze potential becomes dominant. Therefore, the limit is:

lim (r→∞) v(r)² = C
Which means: v(r) ≈ √C = const
